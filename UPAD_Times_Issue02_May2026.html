The Sacred City Gets a Modern Backbone
InfrastructureUrban Development

How Varanasi is Being Transformed Without Being Erased

A $2 Billion Investment in Roads, Corridors, and River Cleanup — With the Ghats Left Untouched

For those of us whose ancestors walked the ghats of Varanasi, the city has always existed in a kind of holy paradox: timeless but decaying, sacred but overwhelmed. The streets we remember from childhood visits were narrow, congested, poorly lit. The Ganga was visibly polluted. The infrastructure that connected the rest of India to this spiritual capital was, frankly, embarrassing for a city of such global importance.

That Varanasi is changing. Not in the way that erases its soul — the ghats remain untouched, the temples intact — but in the way that allows the city to function as a modern urban center while preserving what makes it irreplaceable. The Kashi Vishwanath Corridor, completed in 2021, is the most visible example. It cleared decades of unauthorized construction that had choked access to the temple, creating a grand plaza and pedestrian pathway that connects the temple directly to the Ganga. For the first time in living memory, pilgrims can walk from the river to the sanctum without navigating a maze of narrow, poorly maintained lanes.

Varanasi was not restored to its past. It was restored to its purpose — and those are two very different things.

— UPAD Times

The Ganga herself has been part of this transformation. The river that gives Varanasi its spiritual authority had, by the late twentieth century, become one of the most polluted waterways in the world. Decades of industrial effluent, raw sewage, and neglect had degraded the same waters that generations of our families considered holy. The Namami Gange program — a federal-level river restoration initiative — has invested billions in sewage treatment infrastructure, riverfront cleanup, and factory regulation. The results are measurable: dissolved oxygen levels are up, certain fish species have returned, and the water quality at the ghats has improved significantly. The river is not yet what it was, but it is healing.

Connecting UP to the World
HighwaysAviation

Four Projects That Are Rewriting the Map

Infrastructure That Connects 240 Million People to Each Other — and to the World

Beyond Varanasi itself, Uttar Pradesh is undergoing a transportation revolution that has no parallel in the state’s history. For those of us whose memories of UP include long, uncomfortable journeys on potholed roads between cities, the scale of what is being built is genuinely hard to believe — until you see it.

Jewar International Airport is perhaps the most consequential single project. Located near Noida, southeast of Delhi, it is designed to be one of the largest airports in Asia when complete — rivaling Dubai and Singapore in scale. For UP’s enormous diaspora, scattered across continents, this means direct international access to the state without the transit bottleneck of Delhi’s Indira Gandhi Airport. Families flying home for Diwali or Holi, students returning for summer, NRIs looking to invest — Jewar changes the math for all of them.

The Purvanchal Expressway and Bundelkhand Expressway are equally transformative on the ground. These are not incremental road improvements; they are 340-kilometer and 296-kilometer controlled-access highways, respectively, built to international standards. The Purvanchal alone has cut travel time between Lucknow and the eastern UP districts by half. What used to be an eight-hour ordeal is now a four-hour drive. This is not just about convenience — it is about economic integration. Farmers can get produce to urban markets faster. Investors can reach tier-two cities without losing a day to travel. Small manufacturers in places like Azamgarh or Mau can compete with suppliers in Kanpur or Noida because logistics are no longer prohibitive.

The Agra-Lucknow Expressway, completed earlier, set the template. It turned a six-hour journey into a three-hour one and sparked industrial development along its entire corridor. Real estate values in towns like Kannauj and Unnao — once economic backwaters — have surged. Factories have opened. The lesson was clear: build real infrastructure, and economic activity follows.

Varanasi: The Eternal City
Ancient HistoryCultural Heritage

Three Thousand Years of Continuity

The City That Outlasted Every Empire That Ruled It

Varanasi is not just old. It is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities on Earth — a living testament to cultural endurance that few places can claim. While Babylon fell, while Rome declined, while empires rose and collapsed across Asia and Europe, Varanasi remained. The ghats that pilgrims walk today occupy the same riverbank where their ancestors performed rituals three millennia ago. The temples may have been rebuilt, the city sacked and restored, but the essential function of Varanasi — as a sacred crossing point between the mortal and the divine — has never ceased.

The city appears in the Rigveda, composed sometime between 1500 and 1200 BCE, making it contemporary with the fall of the Bronze Age civilizations in the Mediterranean. By the time the Buddha preached his first sermon at Sarnath, just outside Varanasi, in the 6th century BCE, the city was already ancient. It was a center of learning, commerce, and spirituality — a role it would never relinquish, even as political power shifted between kingdoms, sultanates, and empires.

Banaras is older than history, older than tradition, older even than legend, and looks twice as old as all of them put together.

— Mark Twain

What kept Varanasi alive when so many other ancient cities died? Part of the answer is geography. The city sits on a crescent-shaped bend in the Ganga, elevated above the floodplain. This natural setting made it defensible, economically viable as a river port, and spiritually significant — the Ganga being not just a river but a goddess, the embodiment of purity and redemption. To die in Varanasi, to be cremated on its ghats and have one’s ashes scattered in the Ganga, is to achieve moksha — liberation from the cycle of rebirth. That belief has drawn pilgrims for millennia and sustained the city’s economy and spiritual infrastructure even when political circumstances were unfavorable.

But geography alone does not explain endurance. Varanasi survived because it adapted. When the Mughals conquered North India in the 16th century, Varanasi did not resist; it negotiated. Akbar, the greatest of the Mughal emperors, visited the city and granted it royal patronage. His successor Jahangir did the same. Even Aurangzeb, often remembered for his destruction of temples, could not afford to annihilate Varanasi entirely — the city’s economic and cultural importance was too great. The Kashi Vishwanath Temple was indeed destroyed and replaced with the Gyanvapi Mosque in 1669, a wound that still shapes the city’s politics today. But Varanasi itself endured. New temples were built. Pilgrims kept coming. The city bent but did not break.

The British period brought a different kind of transformation. Varanasi became a node in the colonial economy — connected by rail to Calcutta, Bombay, and Delhi. The British built cantonments and administrative buildings, but they left the ghats and temples largely untouched, recognizing that interference would provoke resistance they could not afford. Varanasi became a center of Indian nationalism and cultural revival in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Figures like Madan Mohan Malaviya founded institutions like Banaras Hindu University, which remains one of Asia’s largest residential universities, to preserve and promote Indian knowledge systems in the face of colonial domination.

The Modern Era

How Independence Shaped Varanasi

After independence in 1947, Varanasi faced a new challenge: how to modernize without losing its soul. The answer, for decades, was that it didn’t really try. The city grew organically, chaotically. Its spiritual infrastructure remained strong, but its physical infrastructure decayed. By the late 20th century, Varanasi was struggling under the weight of its own sanctity. Millions of pilgrims visited every year, but the city lacked the roads, sewage systems, and public services to support them. The Ganga was polluted beyond recognition. The temples were magnificent, but the alleys leading to them were choked with garbage and unregulated construction.

The current transformation — the Kashi Vishwanath Corridor, the Ganga cleanup, the new infrastructure — is an attempt to answer a question Varanasi has faced for three thousand years: how do you preserve what is sacred while allowing the city to function in the present? The answer, if the current projects succeed, is that you separate the two. You build highways and airports and expressways to connect Varanasi to the modern economy. But you leave the ghats alone. You clean the Ganga without changing its course. You restore temples without demolishing neighborhoods. You honor the past by making sure it has a future.

The Silk, the Sound, the Sacred Craft

The Silk That Made Varanasi Rich, the Music That Made It Immortal, and the Crafts That Made It Irreplaceable

Varanasi is not just a city of temples and ghats. It is a city of craftsmen — weavers, musicians, artisans — whose work has defined Indian culture for centuries. To understand Varanasi is to understand how material culture and spiritual life are not separate in India. The silk that is woven here is not just fabric; it is an offering, a symbol of devotion, a marker of status and ritual purity. The music that is performed here is not entertainment; it is worship, a form of yoga, a pathway to the divine.

Banarasi silk is perhaps the city’s most famous export. For centuries, the weavers of Varanasi — many of them Muslim families who migrated here during the Mughal period — have produced some of the finest brocade in the world. The zari work, the intricate gold and silver threadwork woven into silk, was once reserved for royalty and deities. A single Banarasi sari can take weeks to weave and cost thousands of dollars. It is not mass-produced fashion; it is heirloom textile art.

But the industry is under threat. Mechanized looms and cheaper Chinese imports have undercut traditional weavers. Many families have abandoned the craft. The younger generation, educated and urban-aspirational, sees weaving as backbreaking labor with diminishing returns. The government has intervened — providing subsidies, promoting Banarasi silk under Geographical Indication (GI) protection, organizing craft fairs — but the long-term survival of the tradition is uncertain. What is certain is that if Banarasi silk dies, a piece of Indian cultural heritage dies with it.

Music in Banaras is not performed. It is meditated upon. The ustads here do not seek applause. They seek dissolution in sound.

— Sheila Dhar, Musicologist

Classical music is the other pillar of Varanasi’s cultural identity. The city is the cradle of the Benares gharana of Hindustani classical music, a tradition that emphasizes khyal singing — the slow, meditative exploration of a raga. Legendary musicians like Pandit Ravi Shankar, Ustad Bismillah Khan, and Girija Devi all came from Varanasi or spent formative years here. The city’s musical culture is inseparable from its spiritual culture. Morning ragas are performed at dawn on the ghats. Evening ragas accompany the Ganga aarti. Music here is not a concert; it is a ritual.

The challenge, as with silk weaving, is generational. Young musicians face the same economic pressures as young weavers. Classical music does not pay well unless you reach the very top of the profession, and even then, the lifestyle is precarious. Streaming platforms and Bollywood have democratized music consumption but have not created sustainable livelihoods for traditional performers. Institutions like the Sangeet Natak Akademi and private foundations are trying to preserve the tradition through scholarships and performances, but the question remains: can an art form that requires decades of discipleship and practice survive in a world that rewards speed and virality?

Artisan Crafts

Beyond Silk: The Other Crafts That Define Varanasi

Varanasi is also home to a thriving tradition of metalwork, particularly brass and copper vessels used in Hindu rituals. The Thathiari Bazaar, near Vishwanath Temple, is filled with shops selling everything from small diyas (oil lamps) to massive kalash (ritual water vessels). These are not decorative items; they are sacred objects, essential to puja (worship). The craftsmen who make them have inherited their skills through generations, but like weavers and musicians, they are struggling to compete with cheaper, mass-produced alternatives.

The same is true of wooden toy-making, stone carving, and glass bead-making — all traditional Varanasi crafts that are slowly disappearing. The irony is that Varanasi is more visited than ever, with millions of domestic and international tourists every year. But tourism has not translated into sustained demand for traditional crafts. Tourists want souvenirs, not heirlooms. They want cheap, portable trinkets, not hand-woven silk or hand-beaten brass.

The future of Varanasi’s cultural heritage depends on whether the city can find a way to make tradition economically viable in the 21st century. That might mean new markets — export-oriented production, high-end boutiques in Delhi and Mumbai, online sales platforms. It might mean new forms of patronage — government grants, corporate sponsorships, philanthropic foundations. Or it might mean accepting that some traditions will fade, preserved only in museums and academic studies, while others adapt and survive in new forms.

What You Eat in Varanasi

Is Not Just Food. It Is Sacred Heritage — Built Dish by Dish by Brahmin Families for Generations

Varanasi is a vegetarian city. Not by law, but by culture. The religious significance of the city — as a place of pilgrimage, death, and cremation — has shaped its food traditions for millennia. Meat, onions, and garlic are avoided by most residents, especially those involved in temple work or ritual activity. What remains is a cuisine of startling sophistication, built on lentils, vegetables, dairy, and an extraordinary variety of sweets.

The food of Varanasi is not restaurant food. It is home food, passed down through Brahmin families who served as temple cooks and ritual specialists. These families developed recipes that were not just delicious but also sattvic — pure, balanced, conducive to spiritual practice. The techniques — slow-cooking in brass vessels, tempering spices in ghee, layering flavors without heat or pungency — are designed to produce food that nourishes without exciting the senses.

Some of the most iconic dishes: Baati-chokha, a rustic preparation of baked wheat balls served with mashed vegetables and ghee. Kachori-sabzi, fried lentil pastries served with spiced potato curry, a breakfast staple. Tamatar chaat, a tangy tomato-based street snack that is uniquely Banarasi. And of course, paan — betel leaf filled with areca nut, lime, and spices, an after-meal digestive that has become a Varanasi ritual in itself.

To eat in Varanasi is to taste devotion. Every dish is an offering, every meal a prayer.

— Traditional Banarasi Saying

But the glory of Varanasi cuisine is its sweets. The city is famous across India for rabri (thickened sweetened milk), malaiyo (a winter delicacy made from milk foam, saffron, and cardamom, so delicate it melts on the tongue), launglata (a crispy, spiral-shaped sweet fried in ghee), and jalebi (deep-fried spirals of batter soaked in sugar syrup). These are not industrial sweets. They are made in small shops, often by families who have been making the same sweet for generations, using recipes that are closely guarded secrets.

The challenge — again — is survival. The younger generation is leaving. Making sweets is labor-intensive, requires early mornings and late nights, and offers modest financial returns. Big sweet chains from Delhi and Mumbai are entering the market, offering cheaper, more consistent products. The old shops are closing. When they do, the recipes often die with them, because the knowledge was never written down — it was embodied, passed from father to son, from master to apprentice, through touch and intuition rather than measurement and formula.

Street Food Culture

The Alleys Where Varanasi Eats

The soul of Varanasi food is not in restaurants. It is in the narrow alleys near the ghats, where vendors set up portable stalls every evening and serve food that has barely changed in a century. Kachori-sabzi at Kashi Chaat Bhandar, tamatar chaat at Deena Chaat Bhandar, lassi at Blue Lassi (a tiny shop that serves flavored yogurt in clay cups and has become a pilgrimage site for food tourists).

This is food as performance. The vendor is not just cooking; he is demonstrating mastery, inherited skill, continuity with the past. The speed, the precision, the economy of movement — all of it speaks to decades of practice. And the food itself is a form of urban poetry: cheap, nourishing, deeply flavorful, made from ingredients that are humble but combined with such care that they become transcendent.

Tourism has been a double-edged sword for Varanasi’s street food culture. On one hand, it has brought new customers and new income. On the other, it has incentivized shortcuts — using cheaper ingredients, speeding up preparation, catering to foreign palates that expect less spice and more familiarity. The old vendors resist this. They make their food the way their grandfathers did, and if you don’t like it, you can go elsewhere. But the next generation may not have that luxury. They will have to adapt or disappear.
