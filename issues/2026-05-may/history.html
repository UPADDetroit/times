A Living Bridge Between Heritage and Understanding

Every community carries within it a geography of the soul — places that shaped not just where our families came from, but who they became. For those of us with roots in Uttar Pradesh, few places hold that weight more profoundly than Varanasi. It is a city that our grandparents spoke of with reverence, that our parents may have visited once and never quite left behind, and that our children born here in Michigan may know only as a name — ancient, a little mysterious, somehow important.

This issue of UPAD Times is our attempt to bring Varanasi closer. Not as a tourist destination, not as a cultural exhibit, but as a living place that has shaped the spiritual and intellectual inheritance of our entire civilization. The articles that follow approach this city from two complementary directions — one through the lens of sacred tradition, one through the lens of modern understanding. Both arrive at the same conclusion: Varanasi is unlike anywhere else on earth, and understanding it is part of understanding ourselves.

We invite you to read slowly. To let the city settle in your mind the way the Ganga mist settles on the ghats at dawn — quietly, without hurry, and leaving something behind.

Kashi — The Eternal City
SpiritualityKashi

Kashi: The Eternal City of Light and Liberation

Where Shiva Himself Whispers the Secret of the Universe

There is a story told across generations in UP households — that when a soul departs this world in Kashi, Lord Shiva leans down and whispers the Taraka Mantra, the liberating syllable, into the dying person’s ear. It does not matter who they are. Brahmin or Dalit, merchant or wanderer, saint or ordinary soul. In Kashi, liberation is the birthright of anyone who draws their last breath within the city’s sacred boundaries. This is why, for three thousand years, people have come here to die.

Kashi — also known as Varanasi and Banaras — sits at the point where the Ganga reverses her southward course and flows north toward the Himalayas. Ancient texts say this geographical anomaly is itself a sign of the sacred: the river, upon reaching this place, bows in reverence to Lord Shiva, who stands here at the axis of creation. The city is said to rest not on the earth, but on the tip of Shiva’s trident — suspended between the worlds, neither fully in time nor fully beyond it.

Mark Twain, who visited in 1895, wrote that Varanasi is “older than history, older than tradition, older even than legend, and looks twice as old as all of them put together.” He was right in the feeling if not the metaphysics. Archaeological evidence places continuous habitation at Varanasi to at least 3,000 years ago — making it one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world. While Rome and Athens rose and fell, while Babylon was forgotten and Alexandria burned, Varanasi kept living.

Varanasi is not merely a city on the Ganga. It is the center from which all creation radiates. To die here is not to end, but to arrive.

— Kashi Khanda, Skanda Purana

The eighty-four ghats of Varanasi form perhaps the most extraordinary urban waterfront ever built. Each ghat has its own personality, its own presiding deity, its own story. Dashashwamedh Ghat, where the daily Ganga Aarti transforms the river into a theater of fire and prayer. Manikarnika, the burning ghat, where the fires have not been extinguished for centuries — where death is not hidden but honored, not feared but witnessed. Assi Ghat, where scholars and pilgrims gather each dawn to watch the sun rise over the Ganga and feel, for a moment, the stillness that the city has always known how to keep.

The Kashi Vishwanath Temple, home to one of the twelve Jyotirlingas — the sacred manifestations of Shiva in light — has been rebuilt, destroyed, and rebuilt again across the centuries. Each time it rises, it rises taller, as if the city refuses to accept anything less than its own eternal nature. The temple complex, recently restored and expanded with a grand corridor connecting it to the Ganga, now receives millions of pilgrims each year — each one arriving with something to surrender, and leaving lighter for the journey.

For our children growing up in Michigan, Kashi may seem like mythology. But mythology is how a civilization encodes its deepest truths. What Kashi teaches — that death is not the enemy, that liberation is available to everyone, that a place can hold memory across millennia — is wisdom that has no expiration date. It is part of what we carry with us, whether we know it or not.

A Different Lens

The Science Behind a Sacred Experience

What Modern Research Tells Us About What Ancient Wisdom Already Knew

Stand on any ghat in Varanasi at dawn, and something happens that is difficult to put into words. The sound of temple bells. The chanting of mantras that have been sung in this place for three thousand years. The smell of incense. The sight of the Ganga catching the first light. The feeling — documented by visitors across centuries, from Hindu pilgrims to European scholars to American tourists — is of profound peace. Of something settling. Of the noise in the mind going quiet in a way it does not go quiet elsewhere.

For generations, this experience was understood through the framework of the sacred — as the presence of Shiva, as the grace of the city, as the vibration of a place chosen by the divine. That understanding remains. But modern science has also begun to offer a complementary account, and what it finds is remarkable.

The concept of Naad Brahma — the idea that the universe is fundamentally made of sound — is ancient in Indian philosophy. The sacred syllable Om is said to be the primordial vibration from which all creation emerges. In Varanasi, this is not abstraction. The city hums. The constant ritual chanting, the bells, the conch shells, the ghanta — all of it creates a sonic environment that researchers in psychoacoustics have found to have measurable effects on the human nervous system. Certain frequencies reduce cortisol levels, lower heart rate, and activate the parasympathetic nervous system — what we call the “rest and restore” state.

The tradition knew before the instruments confirmed it: certain sounds, in certain places, can return a troubled mind to stillness. Varanasi has been producing this effect for three thousand years.

— UPAD Times

The Ganga herself is part of this equation. The river’s water, particularly in Varanasi, has been studied for its unusual bacteriophage content — viruses that destroy harmful bacteria — which may explain ancient accounts of its self-purifying properties. The river’s northward flow at this point, the confluence of the Varuna and Asi tributaries that give the city one of its names, and the specific geography of the ghats all combine to create microenvironmental conditions that are genuinely distinct.

Mindfulness, now recognized by modern medicine as a powerful tool for mental health, is simply what Varanasi has always practiced at scale. The daily rhythms of the city — the 5 AM aarti, the morning yoga at the ghats, the evening prayer, the unhurried pace of a city that has never needed to hurry because it has always known that time is its most abundant resource — are the oldest mindfulness program in human history.

Our children, fluent in the language of science and wellness, may find in Varanasi not a relic of the past but a model for living well. The city does not ask you to choose between the ancient and the modern. It asks you to notice that they were never as separate as we imagined. That the stillness available on the banks of the Ganga is the same stillness available inside you — if you know how to listen for it.
